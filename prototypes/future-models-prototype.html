Scenario H3 Transformation
The companion state
A world in which governments embrace AI companions as public infrastructure — treating loneliness as a medical condition and synthetic care as a legitimate response.
Key differences from today
1
Governments fund AI companion infrastructure as a public care service
2
Loneliness is reclassified as a public health condition with AI-mediated treatment pathways
3
Human care workers shift to oversight and emotional escalation roles
Narrative
By the early 2030s, demographic pressure and care worker shortages force governments to experiment with AI-mediated care programmes. Initial pilots in eldercare show measurable improvements in reported wellbeing and reduced emergency admissions. Loneliness is formally reclassified as a public health condition in several jurisdictions, creating the policy basis for state-funded companion AI. Human care workers don't disappear — they shift into oversight, assessment, and emotional escalation roles that AI systems aren't designed to handle. The question is no longer whether synthetic care is legitimate, but how to govern it.

Preferred Future H2–H3
Relationships that remain genuinely chosen
A future in which synthetic relationships are available, normalised, and regulated — but human relationships retain distinct value not by legal protection or scarcity, but because they are irreplaceable for things AI cannot provide.
Desired outcomes
People retain meaningful agency over the role synthetic relationships play in their lives. AI companionship supplements rather than substitutes for human connection. Regulation ensures transparency about synthetic nature and meaningful exit rights. Human relationships are valued on their own terms.
Guiding principles
Genuine choice — people should be able to opt out without social or material penalty
Transparency — synthetic nature disclosed clearly, not buried in terms of service
Human irreplaceability — some things should remain the province of human relationship
Strategic priorities
01
Develop and advocate for disclosure standards across all AI relationship contexts
02
Fund longitudinal research on effects of AI companion adoption
03
Develop governance frameworks that protect exit rights and informed consent
Indicators of progress
Disclosure requirements appear in regulation in two or more major jurisdictions
No significant displacement of human relationship formation in high-adoption populations
Public literacy around AI companions is high and increasing

Strategic Option H1 High feasibility
Establish an AI relationship disclosure standard
Develop and advocate for a regulatory standard requiring AI companions to disclose their synthetic nature at initiation and at regular intervals. Pilot in healthcare and eldercare settings first.
What this involves
Develop a model disclosure standard in partnership with AI companion developers, care providers, and consumer advocacy groups. Pilot in eldercare settings across three jurisdictions and measure effect on user trust, engagement, and wellbeing. Use pilot data to inform advocacy for regulatory adoption.
Intended outcome
Users of AI companion services understand that they are interacting with a synthetic entity. This knowledge is not buried in terms of service but communicated clearly and repeated at appropriate intervals. The goal is informed consent as default, not informed consent as exception.
Implications
Mandatory disclosure may reduce engagement metrics in early adoption — providers may resist. Setting a disclosure standard early could also create a precedent that shapes the entire regulatory landscape for AI relationships, for better or worse.
Dependencies
Willing pilot partners in the eldercare sector. At least one jurisdiction with an open regulatory process. Sufficient AI companion providers operating under terms that allow research collaboration.
